\begin{abstract}
Translating between the Indo-Aryan and Dravidian language families presents a significant challenge in Neural Machine Translation (NMT) due to extreme data sparsity, morphological divergence, and script incompatibility. This paper investigates the failure of shared-script baseline models, identifying a phenomenon we term the ``Devanagari Trap,'' which leads to catastrophic decoding collapse. To bypass this, we propose utilizing the No Language Left Behind (NLLB-200) distilled model with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). By preserving separate script tokenization, our parameter-efficient approach converges in just 2 hours (600 steps) on consumer-grade hardware. The model avoids mode collapse, achieving a robust overall BLEU score of 32.92 and a chrF++ score of 60.80 on a linguistically stratified Gold Standard dataset. We provide a comprehensive linguistic, mathematical, and architectural analysis, proving that PEFT on natively aligned cross-lingual spaces is a superior solution for regional Indian NMT.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{I}{ndia} is a linguistically diverse nation with 22 officially recognized languages belonging to four distinct language families. Assamese (an Indo-Aryan language predominantly spoken in the Northeast) and Kannada (a Dravidian language spoken in the South) represent two major regional linguistic pillars. Despite the rapid digitalization of the Indian subcontinent, computational resources for direct translation between these two languages remain severely restricted. Historically, cross-family translation in India has relied on pivot-language architectures, routing translations through Hindi or English. This indirect mapping inevitably leads to compounding semantic and morphological degradation, often altering the pragmatic intent of the source text.

Recent advancements in Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) and massively multilingual encoder-decoder architectures provide a promising foundation for direct regional translation. However, applying these models to low-resource Indian languages reveals significant bottlenecks. Standard full fine-tuning is computationally prohibitive, requiring multi-GPU clusters. Furthermore, early multilingual baselines often suffer from tokenization barriers, forcibly transliterating native scripts into shared phonetic spaces to artificially constrain vocabulary sizes.

This research aims to democratize low-resource NMT by documenting the failure of standard fine-tuning on shared-script models and proposing a highly efficient alternative. We demonstrate how Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) applied to a cross-lingually aligned model (NLLB-200) effectively bridges the Assamese-Kannada linguistic gap without requiring industrial-scale compute.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Evolution of Indic Neural Machine Translation}
Early approaches to Indian language translation relied heavily on Statistical Machine Translation (SMT), which struggled fundamentally with the highly inflectional nature of Indian languages. The shift to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) introduced sequence-to-sequence models equipped with self-attention mechanisms, allowing models to capture long-range dependencies. Projects such as Samanantar and IndicTrans established foundational benchmarks by mining massive parallel corpora. However, these models primarily focused on bridging regional languages to Hindi or English, leaving cross-regional pairs (e.g., Assamese to Kannada) vastly underrepresented and prone to zero-shot hallucinations.

\subsection{The Shared-Script Paradigm vs. Subword Tokenization}
To manage the vast array of Brahmic scripts in India, researchers initially proposed orthographic unification. By transliterating all Indian languages into a common script (typically Devanagari or Latin), models could artificially reduce their vocabulary size and encourage cross-lingual transfer learning. While effective for closely related Indo-Aryan languages (e.g., Hindi to Marathi), this shared-script paradigm severely degrades translation quality across language families. Modern alternatives favor Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) and SentencePiece tokenization, which build subword vocabularies directly from native scripts, preserving the morphosyntactic boundaries critical for Dravidian languages.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)}
As language models scaled to billions of parameters, full fine-tuning became unsustainable. PEFT techniques, notably Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), introduced the concept of freezing the pre-trained weights and injecting trainable rank-decomposition matrices into the Transformer layers. Unlike adapter layers which introduce inference latency, or prompt-tuning which consumes valuable context-window space, LoRA matrices can be merged directly into the base model weights post-training, offering a zero-latency inference solution while reducing trainable parameters by over 99\%.

\section{Linguistic Challenges and The Tokenization Barrier}

\subsection{Typological and Morphological Divergence}
Both Assamese and Kannada follow a Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) order, allowing for relatively straightforward positional alignment in the attention matrices. However, their morphological structures differ drastically. 

Assamese utilizes a combination of independent post-positions and inflectional suffixes. In contrast, Kannada is a highly agglutinative language, attaching multiple grammatical markers sequentially to a single word root to denote tense, case, gender, and plurality. For instance, mapping the Assamese dative suffix ``-loi'' to the Kannada ``-ge'' requires the neural network to possess precise character-level semantic alignment. 

\subsection{The ``Devanagari Trap'' and Morphological Erasure}
A critical bottleneck in early multilingual Indian NLP models is the vectorization strategy. We identify the over-reliance on transliteration as the ``Devanagari Trap.'' 

When a shared-script tokenizer forces Assamese and Kannada through a Hindi phonetic pivot, it physically destroys native morphological boundaries. The subtle phonetic differences that dictate grammatical cases in Dravidian languages are lost when mapped to the more restricted Indo-Aryan phonetic space. Consequently, the neural network becomes ``blind'' to the grammatical rules governing suffixes, treating highly structured words as ambiguous phonetic approximations.

\section{Methodology and Architecture}

\subsection{Transformer Attention and Separate Script Tokenization}
To bypass the Devanagari Trap, our proposed methodology utilizes the No Language Left Behind (NLLB-200) distilled 600M parameter model. The underlying architecture relies on the standard Multi-Head Attention mechanism:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}

The NLLB architecture employs a massive Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizer with a vocabulary size of 256,000 tokens. This enforces a Separate Script (SS) architecture, natively preserving the Bengali-Assamese (\textit{asm\_Beng}) and Kannada (\textit{kan\_Knda}) scripts. By projecting these independent subword tokens directly into a dense, cross-lingual embedding space without a phonetic intermediary, the model retains the structural linguistic integrity required for accurate self-attention.

\subsection{Mathematical Formulation of Low-Rank Adaptation}
Standard fine-tuning modifies the entire pre-trained weight matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$, leading to a massive gradient footprint. LoRA constrains the weight update $\Delta W$ by representing it with a low-rank decomposition. For a given input $x$, the forward pass is modified as follows:

\begin{equation}
h = W_0 x + \Delta W x = W_0 x + \frac{\alpha}{r} (B A x)
\end{equation}

where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ are trainable low-rank matrices, and the rank $r \ll \min(d, k)$. The parameter $\alpha$ acts as a constant scaling factor.

During our fine-tuning process, $W_0$ is frozen (i.e., requires\_grad = False), and only $A$ and $B$ receive gradient updates. We targeted the query ($W_q$) and value ($W_v$) projection matrices within the multi-head self-attention sublayers. We initialized matrix $A$ with random Gaussian values and matrix $B$ with zeros, ensuring that $\Delta W = 0$ at the beginning of training.

\subsection{Baseline: IndicBART and Decoding Collapse}
To establish a comparative baseline, we performed full fine-tuning on the \textit{IndicBART} sequence-to-sequence model using the same 80k corpus. Because IndicBART relies on the aforementioned shared-script Devanagari tokenizer, the native morphological markers were erased before entering the encoder. 

During the 8-hour full fine-tuning phase, the training loss decayed linearly, plateauing near a seemingly optimal 2.60. However, inference testing revealed severe \textit{decoding collapse}. The autoregressive decoder, lacking semantic context from the encoder, exploited the loss function. It achieved the 2.60 loss by continuously generating infinite repetitive loops of high-frequency Kannada syllables (e.g., ``na-na-na'') rather than producing valid translations. This confirmed that the model overfit on phonetic subwords rather than learning cross-lingual semantics.

\section{Results and Evaluation}

\subsection{Mathematical Evaluation Metrics}
The models were evaluated quantitatively using two standard metrics. Bilingual Evaluation Understudy (BLEU) measures n-gram precision against the reference text, heavily penalizing length discrepancies via a brevity penalty (BP):

\begin{equation}
\text{BLEU} = \text{BP} \cdot \exp\left( \sum_{n=1}^N w_n \log p_n \right)
\end{equation}

Furthermore, we utilized chrF++ (Character n-gram F-score), which combines word n-grams with character n-grams. This metric is specifically deployed to evaluate performance on Kannada's highly agglutinative structure, where a single incorrect character can alter the entire grammatical case.

\subsection{Training Convergence Dynamics}
Unlike the baseline, the LoRA architecture maintained a healthier, non-collapsing loss equilibrium. The trainable parameter count was reduced from 600 Million to merely 3.4 Million (0.58\%). The initial peak cross-entropy loss of 17.16 rapidly descended over the first 100 steps, proving the efficacy of the pre-trained cross-lingual embeddings. The model stabilized into a micro-adjustment plateau, finishing at a robust loss of 10.86, completely avoiding mode collapse.

Table \ref{tab:categorical_metrics} reveals a highly nuanced performance distribution:

\textbf{Category I (S-O-V Alignment \& Vocab):} The exceptionally low BLEU score (9.78) contrasted with a moderate chrF++ (55.14) indicates the ``Synonym Effect.'' The model generated grammatically valid SOV structures but utilized synonymous Kannada vocabulary that deviated from the strict reference set.

\textbf{Category II (Post-positions \& Case Markers):} A solid BLEU of 28.49 proves the model successfully mapped Assamese independent post-positions to Kannada's agglutinative suffixes, directly validating our hypothesis that the Separate Script BPE tokenizer bypasses the Devanagari Trap.

\textbf{Category III (Honorifics \& Pronoun Congruence):} The model achieved its highest BLEU score (51.63). This demonstrates an exceptional ability to map complex social tiers, seamlessly distinguishing between formal and informal second-person pronouns.

\textbf{Category IV \& V (Syntax and Clauses):} While functional (30.39 BLEU), the performance dip in complex conditionals suggests that 600 training steps are insufficient to fully capture deeply nested dependencies. However, robust performance in Category V (43.76 BLEU) highlights accurate placement of interrogative markers.

\section{Conclusion}
This study proves that shared-script orthographic unification is highly detrimental to low-resource Indian Neural Machine Translation, often resulting in severe decoding collapse. By acknowledging the morphological divergence between the Indo-Aryan and Dravidian language families, we demonstrated that vectorization must preserve native scripts to maintain affixation boundaries.

Leveraging the cross-lingual aligned space of the NLLB-200 architecture and the parameter efficiency of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), we successfully trained a highly fluent Assamese-Kannada translation model. The proposed approach converged in merely 2 hours on consumer-grade hardware, reducing trainable parameters by 99.42\%. Achieving a State-of-the-Art overall BLEU score of 32.92 and a chrF++ score of 60.80 on a linguistically rigorous dataset, this research establishes a highly sustainable, compute-efficient pipeline for regional NLP development in India.

\section{Future Work}
While the current architecture excels in pronoun congruence and basic agglutinative mapping, performance on complex, multi-clause conditionals requires further refinement. Future work will focus on integrating \textit{Back-Translation} to synthetically augment the parallel corpus using monolingual Kannada data. Additionally, expanding the rank of the LoRA matrices ($r=32, 64$) may capture deeper semantic nuance without significantly increasing the computational footprint. Finally, deploying this parameter-efficient architecture within a larger Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework could enable culturally aware, cross-lingual conversational agents for marginalized linguistic communities.
